# Interview Answers — Degree / Equivalent Experience

Five practice answers for the questions an interviewer is most likely to ask
about education and background. These are honest, specific, and rehearsable.

**Practice each answer out loud. Time it. Goal: under 45 seconds per answer.**
Adjust the phrasing to match your natural voice — do not read from a script.

---

## Question 1: "Do you have a degree?"

**Honest answer:**

"I don't hold a traditional degree. I want to be upfront about that from the
start. What I do have is five years of practical work building AI workflows,
running business operations, and developing real projects that I can walk you
through. I also completed structured AI tool training from Coursiv in June 2026 —
specifically in Claude and Gemini. My strongest evidence is a working Python
project called JobHunterGPT, which I built and can demo live. I applied to roles
that accept equivalent experience because I believe the work is more relevant
than a transcript, and I'd rather you judge that directly."

**Why this works:**
It answers honestly before the interviewer has to press. Mentioning the demo
signals confidence and redirects attention to verifiable proof. It does not
apologize or over-explain.

**Do not say:** "I didn't have time to finish a degree" or "I'm working on
getting one" unless that is specifically true right now.

## Question 2: "How do you meet the degree or equivalent experience requirement?"

**Honest answer:**

"I'm claiming equivalent experience. Over the past five years I've worked
across AI workflow design, business development, hospitality operations, and
real estate project development. The project I'm most concrete about is
JobHunterGPT — a human-in-the-loop Python system I built that handles keyword
extraction, ATS analysis, cover letter generation, fit scoring, and output
review. It's on GitHub. I also completed course-based AI tool training in
Claude and Gemini through Coursiv in June 2026. I understand that 'equivalent
experience' is a real bar, not a consolation clause, and I'm prepared to show
you the work directly."

**Why this works:**
It names specific work, not just years. It treats "equivalent experience"
seriously rather than dismissively. The demo offer is specific and credible.

## Question 3: "What is your AI training background?"

**Honest answer:**

"I don't have a formal AI or computer science degree. My training has been
practical — I've been building AI-powered workflows using Python and Claude
Code, learning by doing across real business automation projects. In June 2026
I completed two structured course certificates from Coursiv: one in Claude,
five hours, and one in Gemini, four hours. Beyond those, the most meaningful
training I have is the work itself — building systems, hitting problems,
reading documentation, and iterating until they work. JobHunterGPT is the
clearest example of that process."

**Why this works:**
It accurately represents self-directed learning without overclaiming. The
course certificates are placed in context — they supplement the project-based
learning, not replace it. "Reading documentation, iterating until things work"
echoes the language in Proofpoint's JD directly.

## Question 4: "Can you show evidence of practical AI integration work?"

**Honest answer:**

"Yes. The best example I have is JobHunterGPT — a Python-based, human-in-the-loop
AI workflow system I designed and built from the ground up. It's a multi-module
pipeline: the candidate profile feeds into a keyword extractor, which informs a
fit scorer, which then drives cover letter generation and package review. Every
module is tested, documented, and runs on Python's standard library. I can share
my screen and run it live right now if that would be useful. The GitHub repository
is also available for you to review."

**Why this works:**
It answers with specifics: module names, design choices, technical decisions.
"Human-in-the-loop" is a meaningful phrase that signals responsible AI design,
not just automation. The offer to demo live turns the claim into evidence.

## Question 5: "Why should we consider you without traditional enterprise platform experience?"

**Honest answer — use the prescribed wording below:**

"I do not want to overstate my background. I do not have a confirmed degree
listed here, but I bring 5 years of practical business and AI workflow experience,
course-based training in Claude and Gemini, and a working Python project called
JobHunterGPT that demonstrates my ability to build, test, document, and improve
AI-enabled workflows.

What I'd add to that is this: the skills the role emphasizes — translating
business problems into AI solutions, building multi-step workflows, identifying
where AI can have measurable impact, and working independently to get things
done — those are things I've been doing with real clients and real projects.
I haven't done them inside an enterprise platform specifically. I haven't used
Salesforce or Amazon Q. I'd need to ramp up on those tools.

But the workflow thinking, the design instinct, and the willingness to dig into
problems until they're solved — that part I can show you right now."

**Why this works:**
The prescribed core answer is direct and complete. The additional sentences
separate what is transferable (workflow design, problem-solving) from what
needs ramp-up (specific enterprise tools). This distinction builds trust
rather than eroding it.

## Practice Protocol

1. Print or open this file.
2. Read one question at a time. Cover the answer.
3. Say the answer out loud from memory. Time it.
4. Compare to the written version. Note what you missed or changed.
5. Adjust the phrasing to sound like yourself.
6. Repeat each answer twice per session until it feels natural and fast.

**Target:** Each answer under 60 seconds. No stumbling on "degree." No apology
for the background. Confident pause before you begin each answer.

**Red flags to avoid:**
- Trailing off when you say "I don't have a degree"
- Over-explaining why you didn't get a degree
- Saying "I'm not sure if that counts as..."
- Qualifying everything with "kind of" or "sort of"
- Volunteering weaknesses that weren't asked about
